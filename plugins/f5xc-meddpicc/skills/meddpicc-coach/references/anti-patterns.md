# MEDDPICC Antipatterns

Common failure modes and evidence-based fixes. Recognize these
patterns early to prevent deal slippage, false forecasts, and
wasted cycles.

---

## Antipattern 1: "We have a champion" (but they won't act)

**Symptom:** You call someone a champion, but they haven't introduced
you to the EB, shared internal information, or taken any concrete
action on your behalf.

**Root cause:** Confusing a friendly contact with a true champion.
A champion has power, a personal win, and takes risks for you.

**Fix:**

- Apply the champion test: ask for a specific action this week
  (EB meeting, internal deck share, procurement intro)
- If they won't or can't act, they are a coach at best — find or
  develop a true champion
- Document what the champion did for you this week in the deal doc

---

## Antipattern 2: POV without success criteria

**Symptom:** A proof-of-value or pilot starts without written exit
criteria. It runs indefinitely, consuming SE resources without
advancing the deal.

**Root cause:** Eagerness to "show the product" without tying the
proof to Decision Criteria and Metrics.

**Fix:**

- Stop. Define exit criteria before continuing
- Tie success criteria to ranked Decision Criteria
- Set a hard end date with the customer
- Require customer sign-off on criteria before kickoff
- Include differentiation tests (prove what competitors can't)

---

## Antipattern 3: Decision Process unknown until the end

**Symptom:** You learn about procurement gates, security reviews,
or executive approvals only after submitting a proposal — causing
weeks or months of delay.

**Root cause:** Not asking about the approval process early enough,
or accepting vague answers like "it should be straightforward."

**Fix:**

- Ask early: "What are the approval steps and who owns each?"
- Map the full process in discovery, not after the proposal
- Validate with your champion: "Has this process changed recently?"
- Build the Mutual Action Plan around the actual decision process

---

## Antipattern 4: Paper Process starts after the proposal

**Symptom:** Legal, security, and procurement work begins only after
the buyer says "yes" — adding weeks of delay to close.

**Root cause:** Treating paper process as a post-decision formality
instead of a parallel workstream.

**Fix:**

- Start Paper Process discovery as soon as the deal has momentum
- Ask for the procurement checklist in solution validation stage
- Stage security artifacts (SOC2, pen test, DPIA) early
- Pre-share standard legal terms for early review
- Identify procurement portal requirements and register early

---

## Antipattern 5: Vague Metrics

**Symptom:** Metrics like "improve security" or "reduce costs"
with no baseline, target, or measurement method.

**Root cause:** Accepting qualitative statements instead of forcing
quantification. Seller-invented metrics instead of buyer-owned ones.

**Fix:**

- Force baselines: "How do you measure that today?"
- Force targets: "What specific number would make this a success?"
- Force method: "Who would validate this measurement?"
- Force ownership: "If we delivered this, who gets credit?"
- If the buyer can't quantify, the pain may not be real enough

---

## Antipattern 6: Checkbox MEDDPICC

**Symptom:** MEDDPICC fields are filled in CRM but contain
assumptions, not evidence. "Champion: VP of Engineering" with no
proof they've acted on your behalf.

**Root cause:** Treating MEDDPICC as a reporting requirement instead
of a deal execution system.

**Fix:**

- Every MEDDPICC field must include evidence (meeting notes,
  customer statements, artifact links)
- Weekly deal reviews must ask "what evidence changed this week?"
- Managers must challenge: "How do we know this? When was it
  verified?"
- Use the MEDDPICC scoring rubric to objectively assess deal health

---

## Antipattern 7: AE-only MEDDPICC

**Symptom:** Only the AE updates MEDDPICC. SE, CSM, and other team
members don't contribute or don't have visibility.

**Root cause:** MEDDPICC treated as an AE reporting tool instead of
a shared deal truth system.

**Fix:**

- MEDDPICC is a team document — SE owns technical elements, CSM
  owns adoption elements, AE owns commercial elements
- Weekly deal reviews include all team members
- Each role updates their elements after every customer interaction
- Shared access in CRM (not private notes)

---

## Antipattern 8: No competitive strategy

**Symptom:** "We're the only ones they're looking at" or "I don't
think they're talking to anyone else" — without evidence.

**Root cause:** Not asking about competition directly, or accepting
the customer's first answer without probing.

**Fix:**

- Always ask: "Who else are you considering?"
- Always ask: "Is build-internally or do-nothing a real option?"
- Research the account for competitor signals (job postings,
  conference attendance, tech stack)
- Plan differentiation tests in your proof strategy
- Prepare competitive battle cards with honest strengths/weaknesses

---

## Antipattern 9: EB engagement delegated to champion

**Symptom:** You've never met the Economic Buyer directly. All
communication goes through your champion or another contact.

**Root cause:** Discomfort with executive engagement, or the
champion gatekeeping access (which may mean they're not a true
champion).

**Fix:**

- AE must have direct EB access — this is non-negotiable for
  Commit-stage deals
- Ask the champion for an introduction, not permission
- Prepare a concise executive briefing focused on EB's priorities
- If the champion blocks EB access, they may not be a champion

---

## Antipattern 10: Feature-tour POV

**Symptom:** The proof-of-value is a feature demonstration, not
an outcome-based proof tied to the buyer's Metrics and Decision
Criteria.

**Root cause:** SE defaulting to "show the product" instead of
"prove the business outcome."

**Fix:**

- Every POV test must trace to a Decision Criterion or Metric
- Structure as: problem → proof → evidence → business impact
- Include differentiation tests, not just capability demos
- Document results in the buyer's language, not product terminology
- End with a clear PASS/FAIL assessment tied to success criteria
