# MEDDPICC Framework — Complete Definitions

## M — Metrics

**Definition:** Quantified business outcomes the customer expects —
cost reduction, risk reduction, revenue impact, productivity gains,
time-to-value.

**What good looks like:**

- Specific numbers tied to a business problem (baseline → target)
- Metrics are owned by the buyer (not invented by the seller)
- A clear measurement method and timeframe
- Tied to executive priorities and budget justification

**Examples:**

- "Reduce incident response time from 6 hours to 30 minutes"
- "Cut infrastructure spend 15% by consolidating tools"
- "Decrease fraud loss rate by 25 bps"
- "Achieve compliance audit readiness in 60 days vs. 6 months"

**Evidence required:**

- Customer-stated baseline measurement
- Customer-agreed target outcome
- Measurement method documented
- Timeline for value realization

---

## E — Economic Buyer

**Definition:** The individual with final budget authority and
political power to fund the purchase.

**What good looks like:**

- You know who they are by name and title
- You have direct access (not relayed through others)
- You know what they personally care about and what they risk
- They will sponsor the deal through procurement
- They can reallocate budget or create new budget

**Common failure modes:**

- "We have support" from an exec who is not actually the EB
- Confusing a technical evaluator with budget authority
- Assuming the person who signed the last deal is still the EB
- Relying on the champion to relay your message instead of
  getting direct EB access

**Evidence required:**

- Named individual with confirmed budget authority
- Direct meeting scheduled or completed
- EB's personal priorities and success metrics documented
- EB's stance on the deal (sponsor, neutral, blocker)

---

## D — Decision Criteria

**Definition:** The requirements the customer will use to evaluate
options — technical, business, operational, and commercial.

**What good looks like:**

- Documented criteria with ranked priorities
- You know what you must win and what you can trade
- You've influenced criteria toward your differentiated strengths
- Criteria are agreed upon by key stakeholders (not just one contact)

**Categories of criteria:**

- **Technical:** Architecture, integration, performance, security,
  scalability, compliance
- **Business:** ROI, TCO, time-to-value, vendor stability, support
- **Operational:** Ease of deployment, management overhead, training
  requirements
- **Commercial:** Pricing model, contract flexibility, payment terms

**Evidence required:**

- Written list of evaluation criteria from the customer
- Priority ranking (must have vs. nice to have)
- Your competitive position on each criterion
- Criteria influence actions taken or planned

---

## D — Decision Process

**Definition:** The step-by-step path from evaluation to signed
agreement.

**What good looks like:**

- Named steps with owners and dates
- Legal, security, and procurement flow mapped
- "No surprises" on timing
- You know what artifacts are required at each gate
- Internal approval chain is documented

**Typical enterprise decision process steps:**

1. Technical evaluation / POV
2. Business case review
3. Security review
4. Procurement / vendor onboarding
5. Legal review and redlines
6. Executive approval
7. Signature

**Evidence required:**

- Step-by-step process documented with customer
- Named owner for each step
- Target dates for each gate
- Required artifacts identified
- Known dependencies or blockers

---

## P — Paper Process

**Definition:** The procurement mechanics — quotes, approvals, vendor
onboarding, security review, legal redlines, signature process.

**What good looks like:**

- You know whose queue it enters and how long each step takes
- You have the customer's procurement checklist early
- You've pre-aligned on commercial structure (term, ramp, payment)
- Security questionnaire and compliance artifacts are staged
- Legal terms are pre-reviewed or templated

**Paper process checklist:**

- [ ] Vendor onboarding steps and portal requirements
- [ ] Security review artifacts (SOC2/ISO, pen test, DPIA)
- [ ] Legal terms: DPA, SLA, indemnity, limitation of liability
- [ ] Signature workflow and approval chain
- [ ] Quote format and PO requirements
- [ ] Payment terms and billing mechanics
- [ ] Contract term and renewal structure

**Evidence required:**

- Procurement steps documented with owners and timelines
- Security/compliance artifacts submitted or staged
- Legal redline status tracked
- Commercial structure agreed in principle

---

## I — Identify Pain

**Definition:** The compelling business pain that creates urgency.

**What good looks like:**

- Pain is quantified and tied to executive priorities
- There is a consequence of inaction and a deadline driver
- Multiple stakeholders agree the pain is real
- Pain is current (not historical or theoretical)
- There is a triggering event that made this urgent now

**Pain categories:**

- **Financial:** Revenue loss, cost overrun, budget waste
- **Operational:** Downtime, manual processes, skill gaps
- **Risk:** Security breaches, compliance violations, audit failures
- **Strategic:** Competitive disadvantage, market timing, M&A pressure
- **Political:** Executive mandate, board directive, regulation

**Evidence required:**

- Customer-stated pain with business impact
- Quantified consequence of inaction
- Timeline driver or triggering event
- Multiple stakeholders confirming the pain

---

## C — Champion

**Definition:** A powerful internal advocate who sells for you when
you're not in the room.

**What good looks like:**

- Has influence, credibility, and organizational power
- Has a personal win tied to the deal succeeding
- Will share internal information and help navigate politics
- Coaches you on how decisions are really made
- Takes concrete actions on your behalf (intros, internal advocacy)

**Champion vs. friendly contact:**

| Champion | Friendly Contact |
| -------- | ---------------- |
| Has organizational power | May lack influence |
| Takes action for you | Is helpful but passive |
| Shares internal intel | Shares surface-level info |
| Risks reputation for you | Won't stick their neck out |
| Has a personal win | No personal stake |

**Champion test — ask for a specific action this week:**

- Schedule an EB meeting
- Share your proposal internally
- Introduce you to procurement
- Provide internal objection mapping
- Advocate in a meeting you're not in

**Evidence required:**

- Named individual with confirmed influence
- Personal win documented
- Concrete action taken this week
- Internal coaching provided

---

## C — Competition

**Definition:** Any alternative the buyer might choose — another
vendor, internal build, or "do nothing."

**What good looks like:**

- You know who/what you're competing against and why
- You have a differentiated win theme
- You've planned a competitive proof strategy (technical + value)
- You know the buyer's perception of each alternative
- You've addressed the "do nothing" risk

**Competition categories:**

- **Direct competitors:** Named vendors in the evaluation
- **Internal build:** Customer's engineering team building it themselves
- **Adjacent platforms:** Existing tools being extended to cover the need
- **Status quo / do nothing:** The most dangerous competitor

**Evidence required:**

- Named competitors or alternatives identified
- Customer's perception of each alternative
- Your differentiation strategy documented
- Competitive proof points planned or delivered
- "Do nothing" risk assessed
